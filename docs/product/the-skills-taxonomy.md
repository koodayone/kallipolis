# The Unified Skills Taxonomy

Among the components of the Kallipolis ontology, the unified skills taxonomy is the only one that speaks to both sides of the bridge in the same language. Courses are described in curricular terms — descriptions, learning outcomes, course objectives. Occupations are described in labor market terms — SOC codes, wages, employment projections. Students are described in enrollment terms. Employers are described in organizational terms. Each of these vocabularies belongs to one side or the other of the supply-demand equation. The taxonomy is the only vocabulary that exists in both worlds at once — a controlled set of skill terms that a course can claim to develop *and* an occupation can claim to require, in exactly the same form.

The taxonomy is also infrastructure, not surface. A coordinator using Kallipolis does not navigate to *the taxonomy* as a first-class entity. They navigate to students, courses, occupations, employers, partnerships, strong workforce. The taxonomy is doing the work of connecting every one of those forms, but it has no direct presence in the Atlas. It is essential but invisible — load-bearing structure rather than a navigable destination. This is what distinguishes the taxonomy from the forms the product section documents: it is not a thing the user acts on, it is a vocabulary that makes action possible.

## The essence

The unified skills taxonomy is the controlled vocabulary through which the Kallipolis ontology reasons about the relationships between its four foundational forms — students, courses, occupations, and employers. It stands on the proposition that skills are the semantic domain where colleges and industry intersect, and that a deliberately constructed, synthesis-based vocabulary is required to make that semantic domain computable. The taxonomy is a translation layer, formed by the synthesis of two ways of thinking about skills — the college's academic curricular framing and the industry's operational competencies framing — into a single set of terms both sides can use.

## The proposition

The taxonomy stands on a proposition parallel to the one the ontology itself stands on. The ontology's proposition is that four foundational entities and the relationships between them are central to the formation of data-driven workforce partnerships. The taxonomy's proposition is that skills are the semantic domain where colleges and industry intersect — the layer at which the two sides of workforce development can actually be compared, measured, and matched.

This is an opinionated claim. Other ontological bridges between curriculum and labor market are possible. One could imagine a credential-based bridge (what certificates or degrees align with what job titles), an industry-based bridge (what sectors employ graduates of what programs), or a geographic bridge (which regional labor markets absorb which college graduates). Each of these would represent the same domain through a different cut. Kallipolis has chosen to build its bridge at the skill layer because skills are the most granular representation of what a person can do, and granularity is what allows the alignment to be specific enough to drive actual partnership decisions.

The taxonomy is what makes the proposition operational. Without it, the claim that skills are the semantic bridge would be an abstract commitment. With it, the claim becomes a computable reality — an actual controlled vocabulary that both sides can use to describe what they produce and what they need.

## The synthesis challenge

Building a shared vocabulary for skills is harder than it looks, because colleges and industry do not just speak different languages — they think about skills differently. The college thinks about skills in the context of academic curriculum — what a course develops, what a learning outcome says a student will be able to do, what program objectives describe. The industry thinks about skills in the context of operational competencies — what an employee performs on the job, what a position description requires, what an employer evaluates during hiring. These are two genuinely different conceptualizations of the same nominal thing, not two labels for a shared concept.

This is why the taxonomy is built by synthesis rather than by mapping. Neither side's existing vocabulary captures what the other side cares about. The academic vocabulary describes what a course teaches; the industry vocabulary describes what a worker performs. The two cannot be matched through translation alone — they have to be synthesized into a third vocabulary that captures what both sides need without being reducible to either one. Each term in the taxonomy is chosen to be a skill that makes sense from the curricular perspective (a course can be designed to develop it) and from the operational perspective (a position can be described as requiring it).

Once built, the taxonomy functions as a translation layer: a controlled set of terms that lets a course's skill claims and an occupation's skill requirements be matched in exactly the same form. Synthesis is how it was built; translation is what it does. Both words describe the taxonomy correctly at different altitudes.

The taxonomy belongs to the synthesis itself — a third vocabulary that neither side authored alone.

## How the taxonomy is built and applied

The current implementation of the taxonomy is a closed set of workforce-relevant skill terms, constructed deliberately after significant deliberation about what terms would serve both the curricular and operational perspectives. The set is bounded and controlled — not open to runtime generation, not extensible without explicit revision, not drawn from any single external source. Keeping the vocabulary closed is a design choice, and it is the choice that makes the taxonomy do its job.

The closedness is a guardrail. When the pipeline reads a course description and derives the skills the course develops, the derivation is constrained to the terms in the taxonomy. When it reads an occupation's description and derives the skills the occupation requires, the derivation is similarly constrained. The controlled vocabulary prevents skill invention, drift across instances, and inconsistency between similar entities. Every course and every occupation gets its skill profile built from the same set of terms, which is what allows the terms to be compared across the two sides at all.

The derivation itself is LLM-mediated. The pipeline uses a capable model to interpret course content and occupational descriptions against the fixed taxonomy, assigning each entity the terms that best describe what it teaches or requires. This is the same interpretive work that came up in the methodological sections of the form documents — principled and improvable. The controlled vocabulary acts as the guardrail that makes the interpretive step safe: the model is not generating skills, it is selecting from a fixed set. The worst failure modes of open-ended generation — invention, drift, inconsistency — are excluded by construction.

The empirical results pass inspection in the way that matters most. When the skills assigned to a particular course or occupation are compared against what a knowledgeable reviewer would say the course actually develops or the occupation actually requires, the assignments are recognizable as accurate. A course in welding gets welding-related skills. An occupation in nursing gets nursing-related skills. The classifications look like what a domain expert would produce, even when the formal validation infrastructure is not yet in place.

The current state is principled and improvable. The deliberation behind the vocabulary is real, the controlled vocabulary is the right kind of guardrail, and the empirical results are defensible. What the current state is not is the final form. Improvement is not about redoing the methodology. It is about refining what exists along specific axes that are concrete rather than speculative.

## How the taxonomy will evolve

The evolution of the taxonomy happens along two distinct axes that are related but not the same: quality and legitimacy.

**Quality** is about whether the taxonomy accurately captures skills. A high-quality taxonomy contains terms that are meaningful, covers the space the workforce development domain actually requires, and produces mappings (of courses to skills, occupations to skills) that are empirically faithful. Quality is improved through mechanisms that test the taxonomy against reality: empirical validation of the mappings, longitudinal feedback from real outcomes, iterative refinement based on observed accuracy. Quality is a technical property — it can in principle be measured, even when the measurement infrastructure does not yet exist in Kallipolis.

**Legitimacy** is about whether the taxonomy is recognized as authoritative by the people who use it. A high-legitimacy taxonomy is one that community college curriculum specialists see as a faithful representation of what colleges teach, and that workforce development analysts see as a faithful representation of what the labor market actually needs. Legitimacy is a social property — it is conferred by the community whose work depends on the taxonomy, and it cannot be achieved through technical refinement alone.

These are different things, and they improve through different mechanisms. A taxonomy can be high quality but low legitimacy (technically accurate but not community-endorsed), or high legitimacy but low quality (community-endorsed but empirically weak). The strongest position is both.

The improvement mechanism that serves both axes at once is collaborative co-construction. Quality improves when experts who understand the domain review the taxonomy and refine it against what they know works. Legitimacy improves when those same experts have meaningful input into what the taxonomy contains — when they see the taxonomy as something they helped build rather than something imposed on them. The two kinds of improvement are not separate activities; they are the same activity. Bringing in community college curriculum specialists and workforce development analysts to shape the taxonomy is how the taxonomy gets better technically *and* more legitimate socially.

This is a different kind of improvement vector than the ones named for the other forms. For students, the improvement was architectural (privacy federation). For occupations, it was an analytical capability (gap identification). For courses and employers, it was better source data and expert validation in the abstract. For the taxonomy, the improvement is specifically co-construction with a named community — the people whose judgment would make the taxonomy both more accurate and more authoritative. The taxonomy cannot be made legitimate by internal refinement alone. It needs the people who will use it to help build it.

## The semantic center of the ontology's analytical capability

The taxonomy unifies its angles around a single observation: the ontology's analytical value operates through the taxonomy. The four units of analysis are what the ontology represents; the relationships between them are what makes the representation analytically valuable; and the taxonomy is the semantic layer at which reasoning about those relationships actually happens. When a coordinator asks which regional employers would be good partnership candidates for their welding program, the question is answered by reasoning that takes place within the taxonomy. The vocabulary is not consulted at the edges of the reasoning; the reasoning happens in it.

This is a stronger claim than "the taxonomy is an important component." The ontology can perform taxonomy-independent operations — entity-level queries, structural relationships, traversals that do not touch skill edges — but these are not the operations that make Kallipolis valuable as a product. The value comes from the bridge between curriculum and labor market, and the bridge is taxonomy-mediated end to end.

The taxonomy is the conceptual center where the ontology's relational logic lives. The work to make it stronger is the work to make the entire product's analytical capability stronger, and the improvement path — collaborative co-construction with the communities whose judgment would make the taxonomy both more accurate and more authoritative — is concrete.
