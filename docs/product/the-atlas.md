# The Atlas

The Atlas is where Kallipolis becomes operationally real to the user. The ontology is the foundation; the Atlas is the surface where that foundation comes into view and becomes movable. This document describes what the Atlas is, what it commits to, and how it relates to the ontology beneath it.

## The essence

The Atlas is the navigational layer by which the user interfaces with the Kallipolis ontology. It is the visual surface that brings the ontology into view, where the act of seeing and the act of moving through are the same act. The Atlas surfaces both arenas of the ontology — the four units of analysis grounded in institutional authority, and the two units of action formulated from them — and presents them as the entry points a coordinator actually thinks in. If the ontology is the territory, the Atlas is the map by which the territory becomes legible and navigable.

## The Atlas is the user's only interface

The user does not query the ontology directly. There is no command-line tool for traversing the graph, no SQL or Cypher console for the coordinator, no programmatic API the user calls from the side. The Atlas is the only path. Without it, the ontology exists as a structured representation that holds together in memory but cannot be experienced. With it, the ontology becomes something a coordinator can see, point at, walk through, and act on.

This is a strong commitment. By making the Atlas the only interface, Kallipolis takes on the responsibility of presenting the ontology faithfully — surfacing what is there, surfacing it in categories the user can navigate, and refusing to expose the user to the underlying graph mechanics. The user does not need to know that students are nodes connected to courses by an enrollment relationship. They need to know that they can move from students to courses and back through the same surface. The Atlas does the translation.

## Visualization and navigation as one act

The Atlas is not a chat that responds to questions. It is not a search bar that returns ranked results. It is not a dashboard that displays metrics. It is a visual surface the user moves through with the eye and the hand. The act of seeing and the act of navigating are the same act.

This commitment matters because it shapes everything about how the user encounters the ontology. A search-based interface would assume the user already knows what to look for. A chat interface would assume the user can articulate the question. A dashboard would assume the user is monitoring something. The Atlas assumes the user is exploring — moving through a representation, orienting themselves, finding what is relevant by traversing rather than by querying. Visual representation is the right form for a domain where the relationships matter as much as the entities, and where the user needs to see how things connect before deciding what to act on.

## The six entry points

The Atlas surfaces six entry points to the ontology, each represented as an iconic geometric form. Four of the six are units of analysis: students, courses, occupations, employers. Two of the six are units of action: partnerships and strong workforce. Together, the six entry points cover both arenas of the ontology and make them equally accessible from the home surface.

The choice to surface both arenas at the same level is deliberate. The Atlas does not privilege analysis over action or action over analysis. A coordinator working on a partnership proposal moves between the arenas constantly — looking at the curriculum alignment that grounds the proposal, then at the partnership being proposed, then at the labor market data that supports it, then at the strong workforce funding pathway that will pay for it. The Atlas treats this back-and-forth as one continuous flow of work, and the navigation reflects that.

The six are the categories a coordinator actually thinks in. The Atlas does not present the underlying graph schema to the user — it does not surface concepts like Department, Region, or Skill as primary entry points, even though those are real elements of the ontology. The six are the user-facing organization of what the ontology supports, designed for legibility in the work, not for ontological completeness. A coordinator does not begin their day by thinking about regions and departments. They begin by thinking about the students they serve, the courses their college offers, the occupations the regional labor market demands, the employers who hire for those occupations, the partnerships they are pursuing, and the funding pathways that will sustain those partnerships.

## The Atlas as map

If the ontology is the territory, the Atlas is the map of the territory. The metaphor is precise. A map is not the same thing as the territory it represents — it is a chosen rendering, organized for navigation. A map decides what to show and what to omit, makes some features prominent and others minor, and imposes a visual language that lets the user orient themselves quickly. The Atlas does all of these things for the ontology. It chooses six entry points, surfaces them as iconic forms, organizes navigation around them, and lets the user move through what would otherwise be a flat graph as if it were a structured space with shape and orientation.

The cartographic metaphor also explains why the Atlas is downstream of the ontology in the dependency stack. A map cannot exist without territory. The Atlas cannot exist without the ontology. If the ontology grew or shrank, the Atlas would have to change to match. The relationship is one-directional: the ontology defines what is real, the Atlas defines how it is shown.

## What the Atlas makes possible

Because the Atlas exists, the ontology becomes something a coordinator can engage with rather than something they have to take on faith. They can see the four units of analysis — the actual students, the actual courses, the actual occupations, the actual employers — and verify for themselves that the foundation is real. They can see the two units of action — the partnerships being identified, the strong workforce projects being framed — and understand how they emerge from the foundationals. They can move between arenas without losing context, because the Atlas holds the whole representation in one coherent surface.

This is the contribution the Atlas makes to the rest of the product. Everything the user does in Kallipolis happens through the Atlas. The artifacts the layer produces — partnership proposals, strong workforce project applications — are reached, configured, and reviewed through the same surface where the user navigates the ontology. Without the Atlas, the ontology and the artifacts would be technically connected but experientially separated. With the Atlas, they are unified into one continuous environment for the work.
