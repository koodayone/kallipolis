# Employers

Of the four units of analysis the ontology grounds itself on, employers are the only ones that are organizations. Students are people. Courses are documents. Occupations are categories. Employers are real organizations — businesses, hospitals, government agencies, nonprofits — that have names, sectors, workforces, and hiring pipelines. They are the most concrete of the four foundationals in terms of being discrete, identifiable actors in the world. They are also the only foundational that occupies a position of asymmetric importance in the ontology: employers are not just one of four entities the ontology grounds itself on, they are the entity colleges actually form partnerships with.

## The essence

In Kallipolis, an employer is a real organization recognized by the California Employment Development Department, represented in the ontology from the workforce development perspective by its description, its home page, and the occupations and skills it hires for in the region a particular school operates in. Employers are the operational target of the ontology. They are the actor the analytical work directs partnership formation toward, and the entity whose institutional shape the college aligns its own institutional shape against.

## What an employer contains

The ontology surfaces four things about each employer, each shaped by the workforce development perspective: a description, a home page, the occupations the employer hires for, and the corresponding skills those occupations require. The selection is determined by what a coordinator forming a partnership would actually need to know about a target organization.

**Description.** A short characterization of what the employer does — its sector, its scale, its operational focus. Enough for a coordinator to know whether the employer is the kind of organization their college's program could plausibly partner with.

**Home page.** A direct link to the employer's web presence. This is the only one of the four foundationals where the ontology surfaces an outward link to the entity itself. Students, courses, and occupations are represented entirely within the ontology. Employers are represented within the ontology *and* connected outward to themselves. This makes sense given that employers are real organizations a coordinator might actually need to contact, visit, or research further. The home page is the bridge from the ontology back to the world the ontology is representing.

**Occupations the employer hires for.** Each employer is linked to the occupations they staff in their region. This is what connects employers to the supply-demand chain at all — without the occupation links, an employer would be an isolated entity in the graph. With the links, the employer becomes computable as a partnership target whose hiring needs can be aligned against the college's curriculum.

**Skills the occupations require.** The skills are inherited from the occupations the employer hires for, drawn from the unified taxonomy. This is what allows the alignment between college and employer to be expressed in a vocabulary both sides share. A coordinator looking at an employer in the ontology can see not just *who they hire* but *what skills those hires need*, in the same vocabulary the college's own courses are described in.

## Where employers sit in the supply-demand chain

An employer in the ontology is the target of an alignment exercise. The four-entity foundation is not just a structure for representing things — it is a structure for *aligning* what a college brings to the table with what an employer needs. Students, courses, and occupations contribute the analytical material that makes the alignment computable. Employers are the entity the alignment is aligned against.

This is a different relational position from the other three foundationals. Students carry skills from the supply side. Courses develop the skills students carry. Occupations provide the demand-side categorical layer that lets supply meet demand. Employers are the actor whose hiring activity gives the demand layer its real stakes. Without employers, the demand side would be a list of occupational categories with no operational counterpart. With employers, the demand side becomes a set of organizations whose actual hiring patterns the college can build partnerships toward.

The ontology represents this with deliberate language. The college's institutional shape — the courses it offers, the students it serves, the skills it produces — is *aligned against* the employer's institutional shape — the sector it operates in, the occupations it staffs, the skills its workforce requires. The two shapes are made commensurable through the unified taxonomy and the supply-demand chain. The alignment is what partnership work converges on.

This is also the prism through which employers earn their place in the ontology. They are not interesting as data points in the abstract. They are interesting because they are the entity the entire analytical exercise is pointed at. The coordinator does not navigate to employers to understand them in isolation — they navigate to employers to act on them.

## How employers are grounded

The methodology for employers is grounded in the California Employment Development Department, the state agency that maintains authoritative records of employers operating in California. EDD provides what we can call a high floor of legitimacy: a state-published source of organizations that exist as recognized economic actors, that hire actively, and that the institutional infrastructure of workforce development already sees and tracks. The occupation node draws its authority from the Centers of Excellence; the employer node draws its authority from EDD. Both are state institutional sources, and both give their respective foundationals an epistemic floor that does not depend on Kallipolis interpreting or inventing data from scratch.

What is distinctive about employers is that the inclusion criteria are restrictive by design. The ontology does not represent every employer that exists in California. It biases toward larger organizations with strong web presence and a societal shape recognized by EDD. The pipeline filters by industry classification codes that map to workforce-development-relevant sectors, and by employee size class with a default minimum that excludes small organizations and informal economic actors. This is a deliberate scoping choice, not an oversight.

The rationale is grounded in what Kallipolis is for. The product exists to coordinate with legitimate state institutions in order to drive workforce development at scale. It does not seek to define the ground level economic realities of California — it seeks to surface those realities coherently to the workforce development ecosystem from the institutional actors that produce legitimate data. Small unregistered businesses, cash-only operations, informal economy actors, and employers below EDD's reporting threshold are systematically out of scope. Not because they are unimportant in absolute terms, but because they are not the actors the workforce development ecosystem can coordinate with at the scale partnership formation requires.

This is the same partial-by-design principle that governs the ontology as a whole, applied at the level of entity inclusion rather than perspective scoping. The ontology represents the community college from a workforce development perspective; it represents employers from a workforce-development-at-scale perspective. The partiality is what keeps the representation operationally usable. A more inclusive employer set would dilute the focus and force the ontology to take positions on entities that the institutional infrastructure cannot coordinate with anyway.

The methodology has two LLM-mediated steps that introduce the same kind of interpretive work we discussed for courses and occupations. The pipeline cleans employer names, generates short descriptions, and assigns the occupations each employer hires for from a regional list — all with the help of a capable model operating against a controlled vocabulary. This is principled work that produces results that pass inspection, but it carries the same kinds of caveats: the assignments are interpretive, the validation infrastructure is not yet formal, and the same kinds of refinement that would strengthen the courses and occupations methodologies would strengthen the employer methodology in the same ways.

## How the methodology will evolve

The future state for employers is narrower than for the other foundationals. For students, the improvement vector is the privacy architecture. For courses, it is the unified taxonomy and the skills association layer. For occupations, it is skill gap identification. For employers, the improvement vector is the *quality of representation* — making each employer more accurately characterized and more accurately connected.

Two specific concerns sit at the center of this. The first is the accuracy of sector classification. Each employer is currently assigned a sector from its industry classification code, and the assignment is mechanical. A more nuanced characterization — one that captures what the employer actually does rather than what its category abstractly says — would let coordinators make sharper judgments about partnership fit. The second is the accuracy of the occupation assignments. Each employer is currently linked to occupations through an interpretive step that infers which roles the employer would have on its own payroll, given its industry and the regional occupation set. The inference is principled but not yet expert-validated, and an employer linked to the wrong set of occupations is an employer that the ontology will direct work toward in misleading ways.

Both improvements are the same kind of work that would strengthen the other foundationals: better source data, expert validation, longitudinal feedback, and human-in-the-loop adjudication for edge cases. None of them require redoing the methodology. Each is a path along which the current methodology can become more rigorous in directions that are concrete rather than speculative.

The narrower scope of the architectural commitment reflects what the form is for. The other foundationals can become more useful by enabling new analytical questions — gap identification, individualized pathway tailoring, taxonomy refinement. Employers become more useful by being more accurately characterized as partnership targets. The improvement is not about extending what the ontology can answer; it is about making the answers it already produces more trustworthy as guidance for action.

## What unifies the four angles

The four angles for employers unify around a framing that distinguishes employers from the other three foundationals: employers are the *operational target* of the ontology.

For students, the unification was the present-to-future gap. For courses, the unification was leverage. For occupations, the unification was epistemic robustness paired with the skills-layer pressure point. For employers, the unification is asymmetry. The other three foundationals provide the analytical material that allows a coordinator to make sense of the labor market. Employers are the actor the analytical material directs the work toward. The other three exist to inform what the user thinks; employers exist to be the entity the user *acts on*.

The operational-target framing explains every substantive choice in the employer representation. It explains why the inclusion criteria are restrictive — if employers are what the analytical work converges on, then the ontology needs employers it can actually direct partnership work toward, and the EDD floor is what guarantees this. It explains why the architectural improvement vector is about quality of representation rather than about extending what the ontology can answer — the other foundationals become more useful by being analyzed in new ways, while employers become more useful by being more accurately characterized as partnership targets. It explains why the home page is surfaced as a property — employers are the only foundational a coordinator might actually need to contact, and the bridge from ontology back to the world is built where action begins.

Employers are the only foundational whose improvement story is primarily about *being represented well enough to act on*, rather than about *being analyzed in new ways*. They are the asymmetric target the other three foundationals exist to serve.
